> [!tldr]
> In the opening lecture of [[Yaqeen Institute]]'s 10-part [[Al-Aqsa Series]], [[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] establishes the theological and spiritual foundations for why [[Al-Quds]] (Jerusalem) and [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]] hold a unique and non-negotiable place in Muslim faith — drawing from [[Quran]], [[hadith]], and the stories of the prophets. He argues that loving [[Al-Aqsa]] is not a political position but a function of [[iman]] itself, and that apathy toward it is a sign of spiritual deficiency. The lecture covers the First [[Qibla]], the multiplied reward of prayer there, the du'as of [[Sulayman (as)]], the story of [[Musa (as)]] dying at the border, six prophetic signs before the Day of Judgment, and what sustained sincere activism for [[Palestine]] looks like.

## The First Qibla — Jerusalem Embedded in Muslim Hearts

[[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] opens with a vivid scene from 614 CE: the early Muslims waking in Mecca in the last third of the night, unable to pray at the [[Ka'bah]] because it was surrounded by idols — so they turned instead toward [[Al-Quds]] (Jerusalem), their [[Qibla]] at the time. None of them had ever seen [[Bayt al-Maqdis]], yet it was seared into their hearts through worship.

[[Anas ibn Malik (ra)]] — the last surviving major companion — described his greatest distinction not by his years of scholarship, but by the fact that he alone among the living had prayed toward *both* qiblas: Jerusalem and Mecca. The point [[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] drives home: [[Al-Aqsa]] was deliberately made the first [[Qibla]] to embed it permanently into Muslim [[iman]] — so that losing it should feel as grievous as losing the [[Ka'bah]] itself.

## The Spiritual Rewards of Prayer in Al-Aqsa

A key hadith establishes a hierarchy of reward: prayer in [[Masjid al-Nabawi]] is worth 1,000 prayers elsewhere; prayer in [[Al-Masjid al-Haram]] is worth 100,000; prayer in [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]] is worth 500 (or 250 as a fraction of the Nabawi reward). Scholars reconcile the different narrations in various ways — some suggest the numbers reflect obligatory vs. voluntary prayers, or that Allah increased the virtue over time.

But [[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] makes a critical point: the number alone does not capture the full picture. The Prophet ﷺ did not stop at the arithmetic — he said: *"What a beautiful and blessed Masjid it is. There will come a time when nothing will be more beloved to a person than a horse's saddle worth of space from which they can simply see Al-Aqsa."* That narration — recognizing the future occupation and the longing of Believers — speaks to the additional reward that accrues to those who struggle through checkpoints and humiliation just to reach it. Their struggle is part of the reward, not separate from it.

## Loving Jerusalem as an Act of Iman

The Prophet ﷺ told a woman who had made an oath to pray in [[Al-Aqsa]] upon recovering from illness: *"If you cannot go, then at least send oil for its lamps."* Scholars extend this: it speaks to any material support — funding repairs, construction, the people living around it, or boycotting those who profit from the occupation.

[[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] draws from [[Surah at-Teen]]: Allah swears by the *teen* (fig) and the *zaytun* (olive) — many scholars say these refer to [[Palestine]] and its blessed land of olives. Unlike [[Mecca]], which was made a barren desert by design (so that people come only for the [[Ka'bah]], not its resources), Jerusalem was made land-rich — a contested place until the end of times, with resources worth coveting.

The theological crux: *to love what Allah loves is a sign of [[iman]]; apathy toward it is weakness of faith; to despise what Allah loves can reach the level of [[nifaq]] (hypocrisy)*. [[Imam al-Bukhari]] placed the hadith of [[Musa (as)]] dying near Jerusalem under a chapter titled *"Whoever loves to be buried in the Holy Land or around it"* — signaling that this longing is natural and praiseworthy for a Muslim.

## Musa (as) and the Pain of the Border

When [[Musa (as)]] was dying, he was forbidden from entering Jerusalem. He asked Allah to let him die just a stone's throw from the holy land — and according to the Prophet ﷺ, he died right at what is believed to be Mount Nebo, at the border, with the soldiers of the enemy in front of him.

[[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] draws a direct parallel: *imagine 40 years of standing at the border, barred not by lack of resources or divine will, but by the cowardice of those who could have acted.* He connects this to the people of [[Gaza]] — many of whom were born and raised in Palestine and have never prayed in [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]] in their entire lives. *That* is the norm, not the exception.

## Six Signs Before the Day of Judgment

In the tent at the [[Battle of Tabuk]], the Prophet ﷺ told his companions: *"Count six things before the Day of Judgment."* They were:

1. His own death ﷺ
2. The conquest of [[Jerusalem]] (which occurred under [[Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra)]])
3. A plague that would take people the way herds are killed (the [[Plague of Amwas]], which killed many companions)
4. Wealth increasing to the point of unsatisfying abundance — the [[fitna]] of prosperity
5. A [[fitna]] that no Arab household would escape
6. A truce with the Romans/Byzantines (or their descendants) that would later be betrayed — under 80 flags, each with 12,000 soldiers

The timing matters: the Prophet ﷺ delivered this prophecy in the middle of war, in a dark tent, with battle ahead. Yet [[Al-Quds]] was the *second* thing he named — right after his own death.

## The Names of Al-Quds and Its Spiritual Geography

*Bayt al-Maqdis* means the House of Purity — connected thematically to *Bayt al-Atiq* (House of Freedom), one of the names of the [[Ka'bah]], where Hajj frees one from sin. In both places, people come seeking purification.

[[Abdullah ibn Abbas (ra)]] noted: *"There is not a single span of [[Al-Aqsa]] except that a prophet prayed there or an angel stood there."* Just as every corner of [[Medina]] carries the footsteps of the Prophet ﷺ — giving it that indescribable feeling of home — every inch of [[Al-Quds]] carries the presence of the collective of prophets and angels. It is:
- The original land of [[Hijra]] — the place prophets have always fled toward
- The land of [[Hashr]] — assembly on the Day of Judgment
- The land of the prophets — from [[Adam (as)]] to [[Isa (as)]]
- A *muqaddas* land — purified from [[shirk]], which will never fully settle there

## The First and Second Masajid on Earth — 40 Years Apart

The Prophet ﷺ was asked: which Masjid was built first on Earth? He answered: [[Al-Masjid al-Haram]]. Then which? [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]]. By how many years? *40 years.*

Two scholarly interpretations exist: the *first construction* refers to the time of [[Adam (as)]] — when Allah built the [[Ka'bah]] through angels, and then [[Sheth (as)]] (Seth), the son of Adam given as a gift after the murder of [[Habil (Abel)]], constructed [[Al-Aqsa]] 40 years later. The second interpretation refers to [[Ibrahim (as)]] raising the foundations of both — one for his son [[Ismail (as)]] in Mecca, the other established for his son [[Ishaq (as)]] in Jerusalem.

[[Ibrahim (as)]] thus raised two of the three holy masjids. This is not incidental: when the Prophet ﷺ first led prayer facing [[Jerusalem]], he affirmed himself as the [[imam]] of *all* the prophets — both lines of Ibrahim. He came not just as the prophet of the Arabs or of Banu Ismail, but as the Seal of all prophets, inheriting the legacy of both Banu Ismail and Banu Ishaq. [[Al-Aqsa]] means "the farthest" — the greatest distance between the two holy sites — and yet they are bound together.

## The Three Du'as of Sulayman (as)

When [[Sulayman (as)]] completed the construction of [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]], he made three du'as:

1. **Wisdom/judgment** that is aligned with Allah's judgment — so he could be a just ruler. This manifested in his famous judgment between two women both claiming the same child: he proposed cutting the child in half, and identified the true mother as the one who immediately refused.

2. **A kingdom that no one after him would possess** — not for personal glory, but so he could use that power for [[da'wah]], to spread the word of Allah. The Quran praises him for this.

3. **That whoever comes to [[Masjid Al-Aqsa]] seeking only two rak'ahs for Allah's sake would leave as pure from sin as the day they were born** — and receive a reward like an accepted [[Hajj]].

The third du'a is still in effect. This is why [[Abdullah ibn Umar (ra)]] would travel from Medina to [[Al-Aqsa]] and refuse even a single sip of water on the way — so that his visit would be entirely sincere and he would receive the full blessing of Sulayman's du'a undiminished.

## Sincerity When the World Moves On

[[Dr. Omar Suleiman]] closes with a call to the kind of sustained sincerity that doesn't depend on popularity. [[Abdullah ibn Umar (ra)]]'s refusal of a sip of water was not a show — it was the quiet internal standard of someone who would not dilute their sincerity even by a drop.

He warns that the cause of [[Palestine]] is already being forgotten — people are moving on, attention is shifting, the enemy continues to excavate the land and erase its people and landmarks. The antidote is what [[Luqman al-Hakim]] embodied when entering [[Al-Quds]]: he described himself as coming from those who *"fight their beds at night calling upon Allah in fear and hope"* — and going to be with those who *"are not distracted by their trade or wealth from the remembrance of Allah."* That standard — internal, not performative — is what the cause requires.
