## How to Overcome Addiction through Faith — Ibn Al-Qayyim's Rehabilitation Program

**Author:** Dr. Zohair Abdul-Rahman
**Category:** Psychology & Mental Health
[Read →](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/how-to-overcome-addiction-through-faith-ibn-al-qayyims-rehabilitation-program)

**Core thesis:** Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya developed a psychospiritual addiction framework in the 14th century that predates Western recognition of behavioural addiction by over 300 years. The paper shows this framework is not only historically remarkable but practically superior to purely materialist models because it addresses the *qalb* (heart) as the root of compulsive behaviour, not just the neurology.

**Why modern addiction treatment falls short**
Contemporary society is architected as a dopaminergic system — apps, pornography, gambling, social media — all designed to maximise craving loops. Western clinical models treat addiction as a brain disorder and focus on neurochemistry. Ibn al-Qayyim's critique, translated into modern terms: correlation between neural activity and addiction does not imply the brain is the *cause*. A purely mechanical model misses the spiritual dimension entirely — including demonic influence and the weakening of the *qalb* through habitual sin.

**Ibn al-Qayyim's foundational insight: desires are not the enemy**
Desires are neutral and necessary for survival — the problem is unchecked desire that exceeds its beneficial bounds. The regulating forces are three: *intellect* (making the right decision), *integrity* (ethical motivation), and *religion* (revealed guidance). When all three are weak, desire fills the vacuum. Addiction is therefore a failure of the tripartite self, not simply a chemical dependency.

**The 50 rehabilitation strategies — two tiers**

*Psychoeducation (understanding the problem):*
- Contemplate the consequences of addiction: humiliation, loss of self-control, spiritual deprivation, worldly harm, afterlife punishment
- Contemplate the benefits of recovery: character strength, freedom, clarity of heart, divine favour
- Identify internal facilitators: sincere commitment, self-respect, strong motivation
- Identify internal barriers: arousal of desire, heedlessness, Shaytan's influence

*Behavioural instruction (guided visualisation):*
Ibn al-Qayyim employs six visualisation domains during craving states:
1. Imagine the joy of recovery versus the emptiness after indulgence
2. Contemplate your standing before Allah and your reputation among people
3. Transactional analysis — you are trading permanent honour for a fleeting moment
4. Spiritual warfare framing — the forces of good and evil contending for your heart
5. Compare the discerning person (free) with the enslaved person (addicted)
6. The heart as a battlefield: two armies, and you choose which one wins

**Five-step practical programme**
1. Build strong motivation — study consequences, cultivate revulsion at spiritual failure
2. Purify the environment — remove triggers, establish protective routines, eliminate idle time
3. Develop willpower through graduated resistance — resist non-problematic desires first to build *sabr*
4. Use visualisation during cravings — combine psychoeducation with guided mental imagery
5. Maintain and relapse-prevent — sustain lifestyle changes, reinforce visualisations, seek community

**Key takeaway:** Addiction is enslavement by the *nafs*. Recovery is not white-knuckling abstinence — it is the deliberate retraining of the heart through graduated willpower, intellectual understanding, and spiritual reconnection. The framework respects both the empirical and the metaphysical, and it works *with* human desire rather than against it.

## Female Scholars in Islamic History — Revisiting Their Pivotal Role

**Author:** Jude Elziq (PhD candidate, Theology & Religious Studies, University of Cambridge)
**Category:** History | ~28 min read
[Read →](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/female-scholars-in-islamic-history-revisiting-their-pivotal-role)

**Core thesis:** Female scholarship was not marginal, exceptional, or modern in the Islamic tradition — it was normative. The perception that Muslim women were passive bystanders in Islamic intellectual history is a colonial fabrication, and engaging authentic primary sources dismantles it completely.

**The colonial distortion**
British, French, and early Orientalist scholars constructed Muslim women as "voiceless" and "passive" — a narrative that served imperial justification. This framing was so thorough that it now persists *within* Muslim communities. Contemporary "feminist reinterpretations" of Islamic scholarship compound the error by treating female scholarly participation as a modern innovation rather than an orthodox continuation.

**What the sources actually say**

The evidence is overwhelming and cross-verified:
- Ibn Ḥajar's *al-Durar al-kāmina*: ~200 female scholar entries
- Ibn ʿAsākir: studied under 80+ women personally; documented 200 female hadith experts in Damascus alone
- Al-Sakhāwī's biographical dictionary: 1,075 women entries
- Ibn Saʿd's *Kitāb al-ṭabaqāt al-kabīr*, Volume 8: 629 female scholar biographies
- 4,000 extant *samāʿ* (transmission) manuscripts from Damascus: 50,000 students — male and female — studying hadith simultaneously in the 6th–8th centuries AH

**The case of Fāṭima bint Saʿd al-Khayr (d. 1203)**
The paper's primary case study: a woman born in China who travelled to Isfahan, Baghdad, Damascus, and Cairo — studying under the greatest scholars of her era, granting teaching *ijāzahs* to students, and documented in eight *independent* biographical sources with consistent corroboration. Her career is not an outlier. She is a data point.

**Other major figures**
- **ʿAmra bint ʿAbd al-Raḥmān** (d. 724): Recognised as a definitive legal authority — men deferred to her rulings
- **Zaynab bint al-Kamāl** (d. 1339): Al-Dhahabī wrote of her — *"Whomsoever seeks unparalleled prestige, let him hear from her; if the seeker of knowledge travels a month for one portion of her knowledge, his journey would not be in vain"*
- **Karīma bint al-Ḥāfiẓ** (d. 1133): Taught hadith in Baghdad to scholars who went on to become the major transmitters of the era

**Why male biographers documented this without defensiveness**
The men who wrote these biographical dictionaries were often *taught by* these women. They recorded female authority not as something unusual requiring justification, but as a fact of scholarly life. The absence of defensiveness in the sources is itself evidence of normalisation.

**Key takeaway:** The erasure of female scholarship is not in the sources — it is in the failure to read them. Engaging the classical tradition restores accurate historical memory and gives contemporary Muslim women scholars not just permission but *precedent*. They are not innovating. They are continuing.

**Category:** Politics & Practical Theology | ~71 min read
[Read →](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/hindutva-and-anti-muslim-violence-how-to-navigate-hindu-supremacy)

It is now state-backed, globally networked, and actively harming Muslims in India, Kashmir, and diaspora communities worldwide. The paper provides both intellectual and practical tools for Muslim response.

**Ideological roots**
V.D. Savarkar codified Hindutva in his 1923 *Essentials of Hindutva* — explicitly modelled on Nazi and fascist frameworks. Core claims:
- Hindus are a distinct, superior race
- India is and must be a Hindu theocratic state
- Muslims and Christians are "foreign invaders" incompatible with Indian identity
- Historical grievances justify contemporary subjugation

The RSS (founded 1925) became the paramilitary infrastructure of this ideology — now the world's largest paramilitary organisation — and spawned the Sangh Parivar network that includes the BJP (India's ruling party since 2014).

**How anti-Muslim violence operates — four tracks**

*Legal persecution:* Citizenship Amendment Act (2019) renders Muslims stateless; Waqf Amendment Act (2025) strips Muslim control of religious properties; "love jihad" laws criminalise interfaith marriage; "bulldozer justice" demolishes Muslim property without due process.

*Interpersonal violence:* Mob lynchings triggered by beef allegations or interfaith relationships; public assaults on elders and children; gang rapes with near-zero judicial consequences. Hindu supremacist leaders openly celebrate perpetrators.

*Institutional failure:* Police enable mobs while torturing Muslims in custody; courts entertain litigation demanding mosque demolitions in violation of existing law; journalists are arrested under anti-terror laws for reporting abuses.

*Digital warfare:* Over 50% of global anti-Muslim X posts in 2019–2021 originated from India. Facebook algorithms amplified violent content. YouTube rewarded cow vigilante accounts. Governments suppress dissent while protecting hate infrastructure.

**Kashmir and the Palestine parallel**
The paper documents Kashmir under occupation using the same analytical frame applied to Palestine: extrajudicial killings, settler colonisation policies, internet shutdowns, digital surveillance, and the criminalisation of political identity. The comparison is structural, not rhetorical.

**Global reach: the Hindutva network outside India**

Hindu supremacists appointed to Trump administration positions maintain active RSS ties. The movement alliances with Western far-right groups (Tommy Robinson networks, white supremacists) — despite being racialised themselves — in pursuit of proximity to white power structures. The paper terms this "seeking whiteness."

**The Hindutva-Zionist axis**
Both movements weaponise minority-status claims to justify supremacist politics, conflate criticism with phobia accusations, and actively support each other's operations against Muslim populations. The alliance is documented and strategic.

**Islamic response framework**
The paper grounds its resistance model in historical figures — Abul Kalam Azad, Ashfaqullah Khan, Maulana Fazle Haque — who combined *iman* with political activism, refusing both passivity and despair. The Treaty of Hudaybiyya is invoked: apparent setbacks can precede transformative change.

6. Amplify Indian Muslim resistance — follow Rana Ayyub, Safoora Zargar, independent journalists

**Key takeaway:** Hindutva is a global fascist movement, not a regional Indian issue. It demands the same analytical seriousness and organised response as any other far-right supremacist ideology — and Muslims have both the historical precedent and the theological resources to meet it.

## Why Silence Is Not Neutral — The Bystander Effect and Moral Responsibility in Islam

**Author:** Dr. Jibran Khokhar (Senior Fellow, Yaqeen Institute)
**Category:** Politics & Practical Theology | ~7 min read

**Core thesis:** The bystander effect — the social psychology phenomenon where individuals fail to intervene in wrongdoing because they assume someone else will — is explicitly dismantled by Islamic ethics. Islam assigns *individual* moral responsibility for addressing injustice regardless of who else is present.

**The bystander effect, briefly**
Coined from the 1964 Kitty Genovese case: when multiple people witness an emergency, each individual's sense of responsibility diffuses across the crowd. The larger the group, the less likely any one person acts. This is a documented, reproducible failure of collective moral agency.

**Islam's explicit counter-position**
The Prophet ﷺ said: *"Whoever witnesses something evil, let him change it with his hand — and if he is unable, then with his tongue — and if he is unable, then with his heart — but that is the weakest form of faith."*

Three points this hadith makes that directly dismantle bystander logic:
1. The obligation falls on *the individual who witnesses*, not "someone" — the diffusion of responsibility Islam refuses
2. There is a tiered hierarchy of response — physical intervention, then verbal, then internal — but *no tier permits complete passivity*
3. The weakest permissible response (disapproval in the heart) is explicitly labelled the weakest form of faith — not a neutral default

**Collective and individual duty**
Islamic law distinguishes *farḍ kifāyah* (communal obligation — satisfied if some in the community fulfil it) from *farḍ ʿayn* (individual obligation — binding on every person regardless). The paper argues that in contexts where wrongdoing is widespread and unchallenged, what was collective obligation becomes individual — because the communal mechanism has failed.

**Wisdom as a constraint, not an excuse**
The obligation to address wrongdoing must be balanced against the risk of causing greater harm. This is not a loophole for passivity — it is a call for *strategic* action. Wisdom governs *how* you act, not *whether* you act.

**Applied to contemporary Muslim passivity**
The article targets specifically the tendency of Muslims — online and offline — to observe injustice and remain silent because others are also watching. The Quranic description of the believing community as those who *enjoin good and forbid evil* (*amr bil maʿrūf wa nahy ʿan al-munkar*) is a defining characteristic, not an aspiration.

**Key takeaway:** Silence is not neutral — it is a choice with a moral weight. The bystander logic ("someone else will do it") is not just psychologically understandable but theologically impermissible. The hadith leaves no gap.

## The Book of Heart Softeners — Dhul Hijjah 2026 Series

**Hosts:** Dr. Omar Suleiman & Sh. Ali Hammuda
**Source:** *Kitab Ar-Riqaaq* (Book of Heart-Softeners) from Sahih al-Bukhari
**Format:** Video series, episodes released daily through Dhul Hijjah
[Watch →](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/dhul-hijjah)

**What the series is**
*Kitab Ar-Riqaaq* is a chapter in Sahih al-Bukhari that collects hadith specifically about softening the heart, detaching from the dunya, contemplating death, and preparing the soul for the Hereafter. It is one of the most spiritually intense sections of the hadith corpus. This series reads through it hadith by hadith, with Dr. Omar Suleiman providing context and Sh. Ali Hammuda drawing out lessons.

**Episodes 7–10 — summaries**

**Ep. 7 — Save Yourself with Small Acts**
The foundation: consistency in minor deeds — a small prayer, a quiet dhikr, returning a smile — has protective spiritual weight that large occasional acts do not. The hadith tradition on *mudaswama* (consistency) over *mukathara* (abundance). Small acts performed reliably build a spiritual infrastructure; large acts performed sporadically do not.

**Ep. 8 — How to Have Jannah Pray for You**
From the hadith: Jannah itself intercedes for those who fast and those who recite Quran. The mechanism — intercession by the deed itself — is explored. Practical implication: fasting and Quran are not merely obligations but *agents* on your behalf at the point of judgement. The episode asks what it means to engage these acts with that understanding.

**Ep. 9 — All You Have to Do is Acknowledge Allah**
*Tawadu* (humility/acknowledgement) as the baseline act of faith. The hadith that Allah says He was hidden and wished to be known — and that every creature is called not to perfect faith but to *acknowledge* the Creator. The episode addresses the person who feels spiritually far from Allah: the beginning of return is not a grand gesture but the simple acknowledgement that He is there and you have turned away.

**Ep. 10 — He Will Give You What You Hope For**
The concluding episode centres on *husn al-dhann billah* — good opinion of Allah. The hadith: *"I am as My servant thinks of Me."* Whoever approaches Allah expecting mercy will find mercy; whoever expects punishment will find what they expect. The episode explores what it means to cultivate hope without entitlement, and trust without naivety. Love is proven by sacrifice — leaving the warmth of the bed for Fajr, giving wealth, seeking knowledge, resisting desire — and every sincere sacrifice is seen and rewarded.

**Key takeaway:** The *Kitab Ar-Riqaaq* tradition is not about guilt — it is about reorientation. The series uses Dhul Hijjah as a season of intensified awareness to bring the listener back to a simple reckoning: what does your heart actually value, and how do you soften it back toward Allah?

## The Firsts — Mariya al-Qibtiyya (ra): Mother of the Prophet's Last Child

**Host:** Dr. Omar Suleiman
**Series:** The Firsts — biographical series on companions of the Prophet ﷺ, now at Ep. 184
[Watch →](https://yaqeeninstitute.org/series/the-firsts)

**Who was Mariya al-Qibtiyya (ra)?**
Mariya was a Coptic Christian woman from Egypt, sent as a gift to the Prophet ﷺ by the Muqawqis (ruler of Egypt) in 7 AH. She embraced Islam wholeheartedly upon arriving in Madinah and became one of the Prophet's wives — or by some scholarly opinions, a *umm walad* (a woman who gains a protected status upon bearing a child). The distinction has been debated across schools of fiqh.

**Her story and its weight**
Mariya bore the Prophet ﷺ his son **Ibrahim** — the only child born to him after Khadijah (ra), and the Prophet's last child. Ibrahim's birth brought tremendous joy; his death as an infant (at approximately 18 months, in 10 AH) brought tremendous grief. The Prophet ﷺ wept openly at Ibrahim's grave, saying: *"The eye weeps and the heart grieves, but we say only what pleases our Lord — and we, O Ibrahim, are indeed sorrowful."* It is one of the most humanising moments in the Sirah.

**The trial of slander**
Mariya faced accusations of impropriety — slander that spread in Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ defended her honour and the accusations were refuted. Her story overlaps with the broader theme of women in the Prophet's ﷺ household facing *fitna* (trial) and being vindicated — a pattern also seen with Aisha (ra) in the incident of the ifk.

**Her legacy**
Mariya outlived the Prophet ﷺ and is considered among the *Ummahat al-Muʾminin* (Mothers of the Believers) in the wider sense, though the precise categorisation is debated. She is buried in al-Baqiʿ in Madinah. Her story is a reminder that the Prophet's ﷺ household was not insulated from pain — it was tested, and the response to that testing is itself part of the Sunnah.

**Key takeaway:** Mariya's episode is not just biography — it is a meditation on grief, dignity under slander, and the Prophet's ﷺ capacity for open sorrow. The series situates her not as a marginal figure but as someone whose story carries theological and emotional weight for the Muslim reading it today.
