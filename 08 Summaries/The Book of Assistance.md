> [!tldr]
> *The Book of Assistance* (*Kitāb al-'Awn wa al-Ma'ūna*) is a compact spiritual guide by [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]], the [[mujaddid]] (renewer of religion) of the twelfth Islamic century, translated from Arabic by [[Mostafa al-Badawi]]. In 32 concise but dense chapters, it maps the complete inner life of a Muslim seeking the hereafter — from the root of all virtues ([[certainty]] and [[intention]]) through the outer pillars of worship ([[salah]], [[zakat]], [[fasting]], [[pilgrimage]]) and the ethics of social life ([[enjoining good and forbidding evil]], [[social duties]], [[counsel]]) to the sovereign spiritual stations ([[repentance]], [[fortitude]], [[gratitude]], [[renunciation]], [[reliance on God]], and [[divine love]]). Al-Haddad consciously wrote in the tradition of [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s vast *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]*, offering an abbreviated yet complete guide accessible to any sincere Muslim. His distinctive framing — addressing each chapter's opening "You must…" simultaneously to himself, his requesting brother, and every reader — reflects a disarming humility: he is prompting his own soul as much as instructing others. The book remains in continuous print across the Islamic world and is widely used in traditional circles as a curriculum for spiritual formation.

## Preface & Introduction

[[Mostafa al-Badawi]]'s preface situates [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] within the tradition of [[tajdid]] (renewal of religion): the [[Prophet Muhammad]] prophesied that in every century God would raise a man to renew His religion for the community, and al-Haddad fulfilled this role for the twelfth Islamic century (early eighteenth century CE). He was born in [[Tarim]], in the region of [[Hadramaut]] in the southerly Arabian peninsula, into the lineage of the [[Alawi sadat]] — descendants of [[Imam al-Husayn]] through [[Ali ibn Abi Talib]] — a family that had produced generation after generation of scholars, [[gnostics]], and summoners to the Straight Path. He grew up in an environment of piety, frugality, erudition, and intense thirst for [[ma'rifa]] (gnosis). He died in 1132 AH and was buried in a simple grave in the cemetery at [[Tarim]].

Al-Haddad's writings are almost entirely devoted to establishing the firmest possible foundations for [[iman]] (faith) and [[certainty]] in his readers. Recognising that people in his time were drawing away from religion and losing their inclination to spiritual growth, he produced clear, concise, and uncontroversial texts. Most of his books are abbreviated adaptations of [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s monumental *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]*, which was itself the renewal work of the sixth century.

In the book's own introduction, al-Haddad explains the occasion of writing: a sincere aspiring brother — a [[sayyid]] treading the path toward felicity — asked him to write a counsel to which he might firmly adhere. Al-Haddad accepted, motivated by Quranic commands to invite to goodness and by hadiths promising that the one who summons to guidance receives the same reward as all who follow him. He candidly admits that he cannot claim his intention is entirely pure — his ego harbours hidden desires — but notes that the one who knows, teaches, and does not practice is still in a better position than one who simply knows and does neither. He addresses every chapter with "You must…," directing it first to himself, then to his requesting brother, then to every Muslim who reads — a choice that saves him from the hypocrisy of preaching what he has not yet perfectly practiced, since by addressing himself he acknowledges the task is still ongoing for him too.

## Chapter 1 On Certainty

[[Imam al-Haddad]] opens with [[yaqin]] (certainty) as the supreme root from which all other virtues branch. When certainty settles in the heart and becomes unshakeable, the unseen becomes as the seen — as [[Ali ibn Abi Talib]] said: "Were the cover to be removed, I would not increase in certainty." Al-Haddad defines it as such power and firmness of [[iman]] that it becomes like a towering mountain: doubts dissolve before it, illusions fail to penetrate it, and [[Shaytan]] himself fears the shadow of those who possess it — illustrated in the hadith that Satan takes another road to avoid [[Umar ibn al-Khattab]], whose certainty was legendary.

Three sources strengthen [[yaqin]]. First, listening attentively with heart and ears to the [[Quran]] and [[hadith]] concerning God's Majesty, Perfection, Magnitude, and the truthfulness of the [[Messengers]] — the Quran itself declares this sufficient: "Is it not enough for them that We have sent down upon you the Book?" Second, contemplating the Kingdom of the Heavens and Earth — reading God's signs written in creation and on the horizons, "until it becomes clear to them that it is the Truth." Third, acting according to what one believes — the sincerity of practice forges and deepens certainty: "Those who strive in Us, We shall surely guide them to Our ways."

Three degrees of [[yaqin]] exist among believers: *'ilm al-yaqin* (firm belief that may waver under pressure) — the faith of the People of the Right Hand; *'ayn al-yaqin* (heart-possession by faith so complete that its opposite becomes impossible) — the station of the Ones Brought Nigh; *haqq al-yaqin* (the certainty of the Prophets and True [[siddiqun]]) — where the unseen becomes fully seen, called [[kashf]] (unveiling) and [[mushahada]] (contemplation). [[Luqman al-Hakim]] is quoted: "Action is possible only in the presence of certainty; a servant acts in obedience only to the extent that he has certainty, and a man becomes neglectful only when his certainty diminishes." Certainty is not merely a foundation — the Prophet declared it to be "the whole of faith."

## Chapter 2 On Intention

[[Niyyah]] (intention) is the very basis of all deeds: the same act is good or ugly, rewarded or unrewarded, according to whether the intention behind it is sound or corrupt. The axiomatic hadith: "Deeds are only according to intentions; each man has that which he intended." The reader is urged never to utter a word, take an action, or decide any matter without intending to draw nearer to God and seek His reward.

A sincere intention can transform a merely licit act into a devotional one — eating with the intention of gaining strength for worship, or sleeping with one's wife intending to have a righteous child who will worship God. Conversely, a malicious intention spoils a good deed: performing good deeds for wealth or prestige corrupts them entirely. Intentions do not sanctify sins — a person who participates in [[backbiting]] on the pretext of merely humoring the slanderer is himself a slanderer.

Al-Haddad distinguishes two senses of "intention": the aim that caused the action (often better than the act itself when the act is good), and the decision or determination to act (which cannot exceed the act). Three situations arise for the person who decides something: he decides and acts; he decides but fails to act while able; he determines upon something he is currently unable to do. The hadith of [[Ibn Abbas]] details God's extraordinary generosity here — an evil deed not committed is recorded as a full good deed; a good deed merely intended earns one merit even if not performed. Multiple good intentions within a single act multiply its reward: reading the [[Quran]] with the simultaneous intentions of communing with God, extracting knowledge, and benefiting listeners earns the full reward for each intention layered.

## Chapter 3 On Vigilance

[[Muraqaba]] — being mindful of God in every moment, movement, blink, thought, and wish — is grounded in the Quranic reminder: "He is with you wherever you are," and "nothing that weighs as much as an atom on earth or in heaven is hidden from your Lord." To practice [[muraqaba]] is to live in permanent awareness that God sees, hears, and knows at every instant: "He knows your secret thought and that which is even more hidden."

When the soul grows lazy in worship or inclines toward disobedience, al-Haddad prescribes a progression of four corrective reminders. First, remind it that God hears and sees. If that does not suffice, remind it of the two recording angels — "he utters no word but there is with him a watcher, ready." If still unmoved, remind it of death — the nearest of all awaited things; if death pounces while the soul is in an unsatisfactory state the outcome is endless perdition. If even that fails, remind it of the immense reward awaiting obedience and the painful torment awaiting disobedience. These are called "useful medicines for the heart's diseases."

[[Muraqaba]] is the station of [[ihsan]] (excellence) described in the hadith: "Excellence is to worship God as if you see Him, for if you do not see Him, He sees you." Every believer knows theoretically that God is always present; what is rare is that this knowledge becomes *permanent*, so that one does nothing privately that one would be ashamed of if seen by a virtuous person. This is the minimum. The ultimate end of sustained [[muraqaba]] is total immersion in God — [[fana]] (annihilation in Him) and consequent absence from creation through contemplation of the True King.

## Chapter 4 On the Inner and Outer Self

The [[batin]] (inward) deserves greater attention than the [[zahir]] (outward), since the inward is where God's gaze obtains, while the outward is merely where the gaze of creation falls. God never mentions the inward and the outward in His Book without giving precedence to the inward. The [[Prophet Muhammad]] prayed: "O God! Make my inward better than my outward, and make my outward virtuous."

Al-Haddad identifies two failure modes. The first is the pretender who claims a thriving inward but whose outward has been corrupted by abandoning acts of obedience — he is a liar, because when the inward is truly good the outward inevitably follows, as the hadith teaches: "In the body lies a small piece of flesh; when it is good the rest of the body is also good, and when it is corrupt the rest becomes corrupt also — it is the heart." The second failure is the person of [[riya']] (ostentation) who perfects the outward — dress, speech, movement — but leaves the inward full of repellent attributes. This is the condition of those who have turned away from the Lord.

A Sufi master is quoted: "A Sufi is not a Sufi unless, were everything that is in him to be exposed on a plate in the marketplace, he would not be ashamed of anything that came to light." The minimum standard is that private and public conduct be equal — behaving as well alone as in company, maintaining God's injunctions and avoiding His prohibitions in every circumstance. This equality between inner and outer is described as "the first step a servant takes on the path of special knowledge."

## Chapter 5 On Regular Devotions

[[Imam al-Haddad]] teaches the importance of structuring one's time around a set of regular devotional practices ([[awrad]], singular: [[wird]]). Time is one's life and life is one's capital — "each of your breaths is a priceless jewel, and when it passes away it never returns." [[Imam al-Ghazali|The Proof of Islam]] is quoted: "Your time is your life, and your life is your capital; it is the basis of your transactions with God, and the means to attain to everlasting felicity."

[[Awrad]] take five main forms: supererogatory prayers, [[Quran]] recitation, acquisition of knowledge, [[dhikr]] (invocation/remembrance), and [[fikr]] (reflection). Variety is essential — not confining oneself to one kind of wird, since each has its particular effect on the heart, its specific light, and its flow of spiritual assistance ([[madd]]). [[Ibn Ata'illah al-Shadhili]] is quoted: "Because the Real knew of the presence of boredom in you, He created a variety of acts of obedience."

For supererogatory prayers: the night prayer ([[tahajjud]]/[[qiyam al-layl]]) is "the best prayer after the prescribed ones" — the Prophet performed it as eleven rakats and urged it upon his community; the [[witr]] prayer (minimum three rakats, maximum eleven); the mid-morning prayer ([[Duha Prayer|duha]], minimum two, maximum eight or twelve rakats); and prayers between [[Maghrib]] and [[Isha]]. Rising after some sleep for the night prayer "constitutes a defeat for the devil, an opposition to the ego, and contains a wondrous secret." The master [[Abd al-Rahman al-Saqqaf]] is bluntly quoted: "Whoever has no wird is a qird (monkey)!" The governing principle: "The acts most pleasing to God are the most constant, even if few." Satan's strategy is to entice the beginner into excessive devotion, then cause burnout and abandonment — so moderation and perseverance always beat intensity followed by collapse.

## Chapter 6 On Reciting the Quran

A daily [[wird]] of [[Quran]] recitation is essential. The minimum is one [[juz]] (one thirtieth) each day — completing the entire [[Quran]] monthly — and the maximum is once every three days, beyond which reflection suffers. The decisive measure is not quantity but *presence*: the reciter should read with [[tartil]] (measured, deliberate recitation) and [[tadabbur]] (reflective pondering). The correct inward orientation is to stand before God as one being directly addressed — responding with awe at verses of divine majesty, with hope at verses of mercy, with resolve at commands and prohibitions.

The Messenger of God said: "The best of my nation's devotions is the recitation of the [[Quran]]." [[Ali ibn Abi Talib]] detailed specific rewards per letter for each context of recitation: one hundred rewards per letter when standing in prayer, twenty-five when outside prayer in ritual purity, ten when outside prayer without purity. Al-Haddad then prescribes particular suras for particular times: [[Surat al-Sajda]] and [[Surat al-Mulk]] every night before sleep (the latter is a protection in the grave); [[Surat al-Waqi'a]] nightly as a guard against poverty; [[Surat al-Dukhan]] on the nights of Sunday and Thursday; [[Surat al-Kahf]] on Thursday night and Friday; the [[Seven Saving Suras|Seven Saving Ones]] (al-munjiyat) nightly; and specific suras in the morning and evening adhkar. The chapter closes with [[Shaykh Abu Madyan]]'s celebrated saying: "A seeker is not a seeker until he is able to find in the [[Quran]] everything that he desires" — the Book is itself a complete sufficiency for the heart that has tasted it.

## Chapter 7 On Acquiring Knowledge

The knowledge worth pursuing is precisely defined: it increases one's [[ma'rifa]] (knowledge of God), makes one aware of His prohibitions, leads to [[zuhd]] (renunciation of the world), and reveals one's own faults and the defects of one's acts. [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s writings — above all the *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]* — are placed first, described as "unique among the writings of correct Sufis in that they are comprehensive, explicit and greatly effective within a short time." Books of [[hadith]] and [[tafsir]] (Quranic commentary) are also strongly recommended.

A firm caution is sounded about books treating obscure metaphysical realities: the writings of [[Muhyi al-Din Ibn Arabi]] and [[Abd al-Karim al-Jili]] contain passages that the unprepared reader risks misunderstanding — specifically, construing technical Sufi vocabulary in a way that suggests [[hulul]] (divine indwelling/incarnation) or [[ittihad]] (union of creature and Creator), which if taken literally constitute [[kufr]]. Al-Haddad cites cases of people who read these books assiduously and "ended up in heresy and unbelief, and talked about incarnation and union." The safe path: ground oneself in [[shari'a]] and the Ihya first, approach high realities only under a qualified [[shaykh]]. The hadith is foundational: "Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim" — the obligatory minimum being what one needs for sound belief, valid worship, and lawful dealings. Those who worship without sufficient knowledge "end up receiving more harm from their worship than benefit."

## Chapter 8 On Remembrance

[[Dhikr]] (the remembrance of God) is "the pillar of the Path, the key to realization, the weapon of the seeker, and the unfolding of sainthood." The chapter anchors in the *[[Hadith Qudsi]]*: "I am as My servant thinks Me to be, and I am with him when he remembers Me: when he remembers Me within himself I remember him within Myself, and when he remembers Me in a company I remember him in a better company." Quranic commands to remember God abundantly are cited: "Remember Me and I shall remember you" (2:152); "O you who believe! Remember God abundantly!" (33:41).

The best [[dhikr]] involves both tongue and heart together — the heart fully aware of the meaning of what the tongue says. After obligatory prayers one says [[tasbih]] (Subhan Allah), [[tahmid]] (Alhamdu lillah), and [[takbir]] (Allahu Akbar) thirty-three times each, completing the hundred with the full declaration of God's unity ([[tahlil]]). The morning and evening [[adhkar]] established in the [[Sunna]] should not be neglected — those who neglect them "and suffer something they dislike, and are separated from their Beloved, should blame only themselves." [[Imam al-Nawawi]]'s *[[al-Adhkar]]* is recommended as the authoritative compiled collection. One should also adopt a [[wird]] of [[salawat]] (prayers upon the [[Prophet Muhammad]]), which forge a connection between servant and Prophet and attract spiritual assistance from his presence.

Perseverance in [[dhikr]] with good manners and attentiveness yields progressive fruits: at the very least, finding remembrance so sweet that every worldly pleasure becomes insignificant. The servant who sits in seclusion, in a state of purity, facing the [[Qibla]], with a present heart and complete courtesy, will see a manifest influence on the heart. If he perseveres, "the lights of proximity will descend upon his heart and the secrets of the unseen will become unveiled for him."

## Chapter 9 On Reflection

"An hour's reflection is better than a year's worship" — a saying [[Imam al-Haddad]] cites without hesitation. [[Ali ibn Abi Talib]] declared: "There is no worship like reflection." A gnostic added: "Reflection is the lamp of the heart; if it departs the heart will have no light." The best time for [[tafakkur]] is the depths of night when preoccupations are fewest. Al-Haddad maps six channels along which reflection should flow.

First: reflecting on the wonders of God's creation — "the inward and outward signs of His Ability" — which increases knowledge of God's Essence, Attributes, and Names. Second: reflecting on God's innumerable favours and blessings, which kindles [[shukr]] (gratitude) and continuous thanks. Third: reflecting on God's complete awareness and observation of the servant at every moment — "We are nearer to him than his jugular vein" — which breeds shame and [[muraqaba]]. Fourth: reflecting on one's own shortcomings in worship and one's exposure to God's wrath through disobedience — which increases [[khawf]] (fear), self-reproach, and zeal. Fifth: reflecting on the transience of this world set against the permanence of the hereafter — "the life of this world is but distraction and play; while the Last Abode is indeed the Life" — which produces renunciation. Sixth: reflecting on the attributes and fates of God's friends versus His enemies — cultivating love for the righteous and aversion for the corrupt.

One firm caution: never reflect on God's Essence with the aim of understanding its nature. "Reflect on the signs of God, and do not reflect on His Essence, for you will never be able to give Him His due." Such speculation leads only into [[tashbih]] (anthropomorphism) or [[ta'til]] (negation of attributes). The goal of all [[awrad]] is *presence with God* — and sustained [[fikr]], joined to the other devotions, eventually produces this presence as a permanent quality.

## Chapter 10 On Following the Book and Sunna

The [[Quran]] and [[Sunna]] are "the upright religion of God and His straight path." Every intention, character trait, action, and word must be measured against them — retained when conforming, abandoned when not. [[Bid'ah]] (innovation) is treated with nuance: three types are distinguished. The first is good innovation — practices introduced after the Prophet's time that accord with the religion's spirit: [[Abu Bakr al-Siddiq]]'s compiling the Quran into one volume, [[Umar ibn al-Khattab]]'s institution of [[Tarawih Prayer|Tarawih]] prayers in congregation, [[Uthman ibn Affan]]'s standardized Quranic arrangement, and [[Ali ibn Abi Talib]]'s institution of rules for fighting heretical rebels. The second is innovation blameworthy only from the standpoint of [[zuhd]] — excessive use of licit comfort. The third is the absolutely blameworthy innovation that contradicts the Book and Sunna outright.

To illustrate the Law's supremacy over apparent spirituality: [[Abu Yazid al-Bistami]] traveled to visit a man famed for sainthood, but saw him spit on the mosque wall — a clear breach of courtesy — and walked away without speaking to him: "How can one be entrusted with God's secrets who is not careful to maintain the courtesies of the Law?" [[Al-Junayd]] is quoted: "All roads are blocked except to him who follows in the footsteps of the Messenger." [[Sahl ibn Abd Allah al-Tustari]]: "There is no helper but God, no guide but the Messenger of God, no provision but [[taqwa]], and no work but to have [[fortitude]] in these things." The "people of remembrance" — scholars who know, practice, and have no desire for the world — are rare but always present, hidden by God under "the cloak of His possessiveness," findable only by sincere seekers.

## Chapter 11 On Doctrine

Correct doctrine ([[aqida]]) must be protected by learning the creed of the "party of salvation": the [[Ahl al-Sunna wa'l-Jama'a]] — those who firmly adhere to the way of the [[Prophet Muhammad]] and his [[Companions]]. Al-Haddad identifies the [[Ash'ari school]] — named after [[Abu'l-Hasan al-Ash'ari]], who systematized the creed of the people of truth — as representing this way, with the note that the [[Maturidi school]] is equivalent. The [[Ba-Alawi]] ([[Alawi sadat]]) doctrinal tradition has been preserved since the ancestor [[Ahmad ibn Isa]], who emigrated from [[Iraq]] to [[Hadramaut]] when innovations multiplied and passions diverged, protecting his descendants from deviation.

For practical study, al-Haddad recommends [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s creed in the first chapter of the *[[Ihya]]* and the *[[al-Risala al-Qudsiyya]]* (Jerusalem Epistle). He cautions against excessive engagement with [[kalam]] (speculative theology) as a means of acquiring [[ma'rifa]] — for [[ma'rifa]] cannot be obtained through argument but only through the path: adherence to [[taqwa]] outwardly and inwardly, meditation on verses and hadiths, reflection on creation, refinement of the soul through discipline, and polishing the mirror of the heart through continuous [[dhikr]] while shunning everything that distracts from God. The ultimate goal — the station of [[ubidiyya]] (servitude to God) — is the aim of the gnostics and the realization of the purpose of creation.

## Chapter 12 On Religious Obligations

The believer must perform the obligations ([[fara'id]]) and avoid the prohibitions ([[muharramat]]), then increase supererogatory devotions on top. The pivotal *[[Hadith Qudsi]]* of the "drawing near" is quoted: "My servant draws nearer to Me with nothing more pleasing to Me than what I have made obligatory upon him, and then continues to draw nearer to Me with supererogatory devotions until I love him; and when I love him I become his hearing with which he hears, his sight with which he sees, his hand with which he strikes, and his foot with which he walks." This is the supreme station of [[walaya]] (sainthood) — where God's servant's every like and dislike aligns with God's.

Obligations are the trunk; supererogatory acts are the branches. Imperfections in obligatory acts can only be compensated by supererogatory acts of the same kind — prayer by prayer, fasting by fasting. Most critically: a person who occupies himself with supererogatory devotions while neglecting obligatory ones not only sins but renders his supererogatory acts unacceptable to God. Al-Haddad gives examples: studying non-obligatory knowledge while neglecting the knowledge personally obligatory; performing supererogatory prayers while neglecting to provide for one's family. None of this is achievable without knowledge — the hadith "Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim" is the foundation. The story of a devout Moroccan worshipper who unknowingly committed a major sin because he lacked basic knowledge illustrates how worship without knowledge can compound harm.

## Chapter 13 On Cleanliness

[[Tahara]] (cleanliness) has two inseparable dimensions. [[Batin|Inward cleanliness]] means purifying the soul from vile traits — [[kibr]] (pride), [[riya']] (ostentation), [[hasad]] (envy), love of the world — and adorning it with noble traits: humility, modesty, sincerity, and generosity. [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s second volume of the *[[Ihya]]* treats this in depth. Outward cleanliness means avoiding transgressions and persevering in acts of obedience.

Among the physical [[Sunna]] practices: removing public hair and clipping nails (not letting them exceed forty days), using the [[miswak]] (tooth-stick, preferably arak wood) before every act of worship, wearing [[kohl]] in each eye nightly as the Prophet did, using perfume abundantly. For [[wudu]] (ritual ablution): renew it before each prayer; "[[wudu]] is the weapon of the believer, and when the weapon is in evidence, the enemy dares not approach." For [[ghusl]] (full ritual bath): perform it immediately upon [[janaba]] (major impurity) — "the angels do not enter a house where there is a person in a state of janaba, and when the angels go the devils come." Sleep and eating in a state of janaba is strongly discouraged.

The famous story of the student of [[Shaykh Abu'l-Hasan al-Shadhili]] encapsulates this chapter's spirit: he spent a year renewing his [[wudu]] after every break and praying two rakats each time. At the year's end he drew a bucket of gold from a well, poured it back with no desire for it, and was made a "summoner to God" by the Shaykh — who told him: "You have now become all alchemy." The moral: sustained ritual purity transforms the servant at a depth that no external discipline can match.

## Chapter 14 On Following the Sunna

The Prophetic way ([[Sunna]]) covers not only formal worship but every dimension of daily life. Al-Haddad covers in detail: **clothing** (right sleeve first, moderation in length, turban, avoiding vanity and excess); **speech** (clarity, not interrupting, avoiding [[lying]], [[backbiting]] ([[ghiba]]), [[slander]] ([[namima]]), and excessive laughter which "kills the heart"); **walking** (with energy but not conceit, not turning around out of curiosity — the Prophet "used just to stop but not turn around" when called); **sitting** (facing the [[Qibla]], controlling posture, the du'a of forgiveness before rising); **eating** (bismillah, right hand, small morsels, eating from the sides not the center of the bowl — "the center is where the baraka descends"; licking fingers, praising God after, never criticizing food); **drinking** (sipping, three breaths, not breathing into the cup); **sleeping** (on the right side facing the Qibla, specific du'as, not sleeping after [[Fajr]] or after [[Asr]]).

The spiritual principle: the one who observes Prophetic etiquette in everyday activities "is preserved by God from trespassing into the base attributes and behaviour that are outside them, and obtains the religious and worldly benefits that God has set, through His wisdom, within them." Every daily act becomes a devotion when performed with correct [[niyyah]] (intention) and Sunna form. Begin everything with the Name of God — this is the universal key.

## Chapter 15 On Conduct in Mosques

Mosques are the houses of God and the places most beloved to Him on earth. Al-Haddad urges prolonged stays with the intention of [[i'tikaf]] (withdrawal/seclusion). "The mosque is the house of all who have [[taqwa]]." Among the seven shaded beneath God's Throne on the Day when no shade exists except His is "a man whose heart remained attached to the mosque from the moment he left it until his return." While in the mosque: complete respect, no worldly talk, occupation exclusively with worship — "it was built only for the worship of God."

Entering etiquette: right foot first, the specific du'a seeking God's mercy and blessings upon the Prophet, then two rakats [[tahiyyat al-masjid]] (greeting of the mosque) before sitting. Exiting: left foot first, the du'a changed to "gates of Your favour." For the [[adhan]] (call to prayer): repeat what the [[mu'adhdhin]] says except at "Hayya 'ala al-Salah/Falah" — respond with "La hawla wa la quwwata illa billah." When the adhan finishes, bless the Prophet and say the specific du'a granting him "the Intercession and superiority." Pray as much as possible between the [[adhan]] and [[iqama]] — "prayers uttered between the two calls are never refused." Among the du'as recommended for this time: asking God for well-being ([[aafiya]]) in this world and the next.

## Chapter 16 On the Ritual Prayer

Praying at the *beginning* of the prayer period is superior to its end "like the superiority of the hereafter over this world." Al-Haddad urges performing [[wudu]] and arriving at the mosque by the time of the [[adhan]]. The regular Sunna rakats before and after obligatory prayers should never be missed from negligence; if missed, make them up as soon as possible. The prayer must have correct form and present heart together — "the man in prayer is communing with his Lord."

A prayer offered with attentiveness emerges white and glowing, ascending to say "may God safeguard you as you have safeguarded me." One offered heedlessly emerges black and says "may God neglect you as you neglected me." [[Satan]] specifically targets the believer the moment he stands for prayer, opening the doors of worldly thoughts. [[Al-Hasan al-Basri]] is quoted: "Every prayer in which the heart is not attentive is nearer to punishment than reward." The hadith: "Only that of which the servant is conscious during prayer is credited to him." The believer should vary the suras recited, not confining himself to the same short ones; when leading a congregation he should shorten out of mercy for those with pressing needs. The last prayer the Prophet led in congregation was the sunset prayer in which he recited Surat al-Mursalat.

## Chapter 17 On Prayer in Congregation

Following the [[imam]] properly means never preceding him and never moving simultaneously — only following just after. The first row carries the greatest blessing: "God and His angels bless the foremost row." Rows must be straight and gapless: "I see Satan penetrating the gap in the row just as a small lamb might do." Close the gaps and press shoulder to shoulder, for disagreement in the rows leads to disagreement in the hearts. The congregational prayer is twenty-seven times better than the individual prayer.

Abandoning the collective prayer without valid excuse is among the gravest negligences — the Prophet reportedly threatened to burn the houses of those who persistently stayed away. The story of the Companions supporting men too ill to stand unaided so they could take their place in the rows — rather than miss the congregation — illustrates the seriousness with which the early community regarded this. For the [[Jumu'a|Friday prayer]]: missing three consecutive times due to negligence seals the heart. Arrive early — before the sun reaches its zenith — sit near the pulpit, and listen to the [[khutba]] in complete silence; idle talk or aimless thought during it voids its merit. Command all dependents — wife, children, servants — to establish the obligatory prayers: "The bond between us and them is the prayer; whoever abandons it has committed idolatry."

## Chapter 18 On Zakat

[[Zakat]] must be given willingly, on time, and purely for God — separated from one's wealth before distribution. Devising legal tricks to evade it (deceptive gifting, fragmenting wealth before the year completes) stores only punishment; God punishes such people through their wealth in this world, "and the torment of the hereafter is even greater." [[Abu Bakr al-Siddiq]] waged war on those who refused to pay zakat after the Prophet's death, declaring them apostates — equating it in weight with the prayer itself. Give [[zakat al-fitr]] at the end of [[Ramadan]] for oneself and all in one's provision.

Beyond obligatory zakat, al-Haddad urges generous voluntary [[sadaqa]] (charity): give something daily, even a small amount, and give early. Secret charity is seventy times better than public charity — it extinguishes God's wrath and is safe from the [[riya']] (ostentation) that ruins deeds. "Hardships do not cross the barrier of charity." Never disappoint a beggar at the door — he is a gift from God. Give with a smile, aware that the reward is incomparably greater than the gift — a single morsel given sincerely may outweigh [[Uhud|Mount Uhud]] in reward. The fear of poverty must not prevent giving: "It is the abandonment of charity which brings on poverty; charity on the contrary attracts wealth." Charity immediately increases provision, lengthens life, protects from an evil death, and gives bodily health; in the hereafter it extinguishes sins, shades the giver on the Day of Rising, and protects from punishment.

## Chapter 19 On Fasting

[[Ramadan]] is the supreme month: a supererogatory act in it equals an obligatory act at any other time; the gates of the Fire are shut, the gates of the Garden opened, and the devils shackled. Al-Haddad urges working only for the hereafter during this month, arranging worldly affairs before it arrives so as to be free for worship. Intensify especially in the last ten nights; if able, do not leave the mosque except when strictly necessary during them — this is the spirit of [[i'tikaf]]. The [[Tarawih Prayer|Tarawih]] prayer should be performed each night; the early community completed the entire [[Quran]] within it over the month, reciting one part per night.

Watch carefully for [[Laylat al-Qadr]] (Night of Destiny) — "better than a thousand months" — most likely in the odd-numbered nights of the last ten. Break the fast ([[iftar]]) promptly at sunset; delay the pre-dawn meal ([[suhur]]) close to dawn. Feed others when they break their fast — the one who feeds a fasting person earns the same reward as the faster without diminishing it. Recommended voluntary fasts: the Day of [[Arafat]] (expiates two years of sins for the non-pilgrim), [[Ashura]] with the ninth of Muharram, six days of [[Shawwal]], three White Days monthly (13th, 14th, 15th of each lunar month), and Mondays and Thursdays. The *[[Hadith Qudsi]]*: "All good deeds of the son of Adam are multiplied ten to seven hundredfold, except for fasting — for it is Mine, and I shall reward him for it — he has left his appetite, his food and drink for My sake." Fasting is "the pillar of discipline and constitutes half of [[fortitude]]."

## Chapter 20 On Pilgrimage

Hasten to perform [[Hajj]] and [[Umra]] as soon as one becomes able — do not postpone. The prophetic warning is stark: "The one who is not hindered by an obvious necessity, a disabling sickness, or a tyrannical ruler, and who dies without having performed the Hajj, let him die a Jew if he so chooses, or a Christian if he so chooses." A divine saying warns that God considers an evil servant one whose body He made healthy and wealth abundant, yet who lets five years pass without coming to His House. Perform supererogatory [[Hajj]] and [[Umra]] when able — each is an occasion of immense reward.

Before traveling: learn all the rites, Sunna acts, and invocations; purify the intention — do not mix [[Hajj]] with commercial intent, or allow trade to distract from the correct performance of the rites ordained by God. After [[Hajj]] at [[Mecca]], visiting the [[Prophet Muhammad]]'s grave in [[Madinah]] is effectively obligatory for one who traveled this far — it is churlish to visit God's House while neglecting to visit God's Beloved: "Visiting him after his death is like visiting him during his life; he is alive in his grave as are all the Prophets." The chapter also covers: [[istikharah]] prayer before major decisions — "the one who does istikharah never fails, and the one who consults others never regrets it"; making and fulfilling [[vows]] ([[nadhr]]) promptly and avoiding frequent vowing; the consequences of false oaths (return wrongly-seized property and expiate); and the grave sin of false testimony, which the Prophet ranked alongside idolatry.

## Chapter 21 On Scrupulousness

[[Wara']] (scrupulousness in avoiding the prohibited and doubtful) is "the pillar of religion" and the emphasis of the practicing scholars. Hadith foundation: "All flesh that has grown on wrongfully acquired money, the Fire has first right to it." And: "The one who guards himself against suspect things has protected his religion and his honour; the one who falls into suspect things will fall into the prohibited." Those who subsist on prohibited or suspect things are seldom granted genuine success in good deeds — when their deeds appear good they are inwardly tarnished by [[ujub]] (self-admiration) and [[riya']] (ostentation).

[[Abd-Allah ibn Umar]] declared: "If you pray until your backs become bent and fast until you become like strings, God will only accept this if done with a safeguarding scrupulousness." A hadith states that wearing even a garment one-tenth of whose price is illicit prevents one's prayers from being accepted — how much more completely illicit food, which infiltrates the entire body and generates the energy for every action?

Three ways religious people fall into ambiguity: (1) not thoroughly investigating the source of what they receive — al-Haddad provides a three-tier framework for dealing with people (known virtuous, unknown, known wrongdoers); (2) not safeguarding against invalid transactions — avoiding [[riba]] (usury), cheating, lying about merchandise; (3) being engrossed in worldly cravings, which narrows the lawful. [[Ibrahim ibn Adham]] warned: "If you eat well you will no longer feel obliged to pray at night and fast by day." The principle: lawful things do not bear extravagance. Scrupulousness becomes easy when one wants from the world only what is necessary.

## Chapter 22 On Enjoining Good and Forbidding Evil

[[Amr bil-ma'ruf wa nahy an al-munkar]] — enjoining good and forbidding evil — is "the pivot around which religion revolves, and the reason God revealed His Books and sent His Messengers." The Quran cursed those among the [[Banu Isra'il|Children of Israel]] who did not restrain each other from wickedness. The tri-level hadith: "Anyone who sees a reprehensible thing should change it with his hand; if unable, with his tongue; if unable, with his heart — which is the weakest degree of faith."

It is a [[fard kifaya]] (collective obligation): if some discharge it, others are exempt; if none do, all capable and aware people are held responsible. The sequence of escalation: inform, then counsel, then — if unheeded — coerce and break forbidden instruments (pour away wine, restore illicit money). The final level belongs only to those wholly dedicated to God or authorised by the ruler. Silence is permitted when one knows with certainty that words will not be accepted or that obvious harm will result. But silence from fear of losing position, money, or benefit from the wrongdoer is [[dissimulation]] — a crime. Enjoining and forbidding must be done sincerely for God, gently, wisely, and with compassion: "These attributes do not combine in one person... but that his words become effective and evoke reverence." Beware of spying on Muslims' private affairs. When evil becomes rampant and incorrigible in one's town, isolation or emigration offers safety.

## Chapter 23 On Social Duties

Every person has two categories of charges. "Personal" charges are the seven organs — tongue, ears, eyes, stomach, genitals, hands, feet — trusts from God that He created only for obedience; using them in disobedience turns His favour into ingratitude. They will testify on the Day of Judgment for or against the servant. "Public" charges are all in one's custody — family, wife, children — whom one must guide to performing obligations and avoiding prohibitions.

Treat those in one's care with both **justice** (their rights in provision, clothing, fair treatment; taking the wronged one's rights from the unjust among them) and **graciousness** (gentleness, nobility, occasional laughter without sin, removing estrangement while maintaining respect). Forgive their wrongs against you — what they embezzle from your wealth you will find on the side of good deeds in the Balance. The Messenger said to forgive servants seventy mistakes per day.

**Parents**: loyalty ([[birr al-walidayn]]) is a most certain duty — disloyalty is among the greatest sins. The Quran pairs the worship of God with the excellent treatment of parents in the same verse. **Kinship ties** ([[silat al-rahim]]): "The maintainer is not him who rewards kindness; rather he is the man who, when his kinship ties are severed, joins them again." **Neighbors**: Gabriel commanded the Prophet to be kind to the neighbor so insistently that he thought the neighbor might be allowed to inherit. **Loving and hating for God's sake** — not for personal reasons — is "one of the firmest handholds of faith." Choose righteous companions: "A man's religion is that of his intimate."

## Chapter 24 On Kindness and Charity

Active goodness ([[ihsan]]) to others is grounded in the hadith: "God shall grant ease to him who eases the hardship of another; shall conceal the sins of him who conceals the disgrace of a believer; shall relieve from one of the hardships of the Day of Rising the one who relieves a Muslim from one of the hardships of this world." Remove harm from the road — a man was granted the Garden because he removed a thorny branch from the Muslims' path. Stroke the orphan's head — God records ten good deeds per hair touched.

Intercede on behalf of people with those in authority — "God will ask His servant to account for his influence just as He will ask him to account for his money" — but intercession to deflect canonical [[hudud]] (punishments) is forbidden. Lend generously — "the reward for a loan exceeds that for charity eight times," since only someone truly in need borrows. Console the grief-stricken — the one who helps another endure calamity patiently receives a reward similar to the sufferer's. Do not [[gloating|gloat]] at another Muslim's misfortune — "lest God release him and afflict you." Do not openly revile a Muslim for a sin — the one who does so will be similarly afflicted before he dies. Try to gladden the hearts of believers in every permissible way.

## Chapter 25 On Counsel

[[Nasiha]] (sincere counsel) is the substance of religion — the hadith declares: "Religion is nasiha." When asked "For whom?", the Prophet replied: "For God, His Book, His Messenger, the leaders of the Muslims, and ordinary Muslims." Counsel for God means serving Him sincerely with one's entire being; for His Book: reading, reflecting, implementing, and spreading it; for the Messenger: following his [[Sunna]]; for the leaders: advising sincerely without flattery; for ordinary Muslims: wanting for them what one wants for oneself.

The conditions for effective [[nasiha]]: sincerity (not seeking a reputation for being the one who gave advice), gentleness (not harshness or humiliation), privacy when possible, and orientation toward the person's genuine benefit. The one who receives counsel should accept it gratefully. Al-Haddad's own framing of this entire book as counsel addressed simultaneously to himself, his requesting brother, and every reader brings this section of social ethics full circle — [[nasiha]] is not a duty discharged occasionally; it is a continuous orientation of care toward God's servants.

## Chapter 26 On Salutations and a Salutary Opinion

Spreading the [[salam]] ("As-salamu alaykum") widely is a Prophetic instruction linked to love and faith: "You will not enter the Garden until you believe, and you will not believe until you love one another; shall I not inform you of something that, if you did it, you would love one another? Spread the salam among you." Al-Haddad urges greeting all Muslims known and unknown, initiating the greeting, and even greeting when entering one's own home if alone — an angel responds. The Sunna protocols for precedence (younger to elder, rider to pedestrian, smaller group to larger) reflect the spirit of mutual honor.

[[Husn al-dhan]] (good opinion of fellow Muslims) is the chapter's paired theme: a believer interprets his brother's acts in the best possible light. Spying and prying into others' private affairs is forbidden — "The one who seeks out the secrets of his brother Muslim will have God seeking out his secrets until He exposes him, even should he be in the depths of his house." Assuming the worst about another Muslim when evidence is lacking is itself a sin. The believer should habituate himself to positive interpretation; the Quran commands: "Refrain from most suspicion, for some suspicion is sin."

## Chapter 27 On Repentance Hope and Fear

[[Tawba]] (repentance) is obligatory immediately upon every sin — no license for delay. Its conditions: ceasing the sin completely, feeling genuine remorse, firmly resolving not to return, and — if the sin involved another person's right — restoring that right or seeking forgiveness from the wronged party. The Quran declares: "God loves those who repent." The *[[Hadith Qudsi]]*: "O My servants! You do wrong night and day, and I forgive all sins — so seek My forgiveness and I shall forgive you." No sin is too great for God's forgiveness when the servant returns sincerely.

[[Khawf]] (fear) and [[raja']] (hope) are the two wings of the believer — both are necessary, and losing either is dangerous. Fear without hope leads to [[despair]] ([[qunut]]) of God's mercy — itself a greater sin. Hope without fear leads to false security ([[aman min makr Allah]]) — feeling safe from God's plan — equally condemned. The balance: fear should dominate while the servant is healthy and fully capable; hope should dominate at the approach of death, so that he meets God with good expectation. Al-Haddad is characteristically candid about himself: "I seek God's forgiveness, and do not claim that my intention in writing this treatise is confined to good religious purposes — I am aware of the hidden desires, egotistic passions, and worldly wishes that I harbour." His very honesty is itself the model of tawba.

## Chapter 28 On Fortitude

[[Sabr]] (fortitude/patience) has three forms: patience in obeying God — the most noble, requiring sustained effort against the ego's preference for ease; patience in abstaining from what God has forbidden — resisting temptation; and patience in enduring what God has decreed — calamities and hardships not chosen. The Quran promises: "Indeed those who have fortitude will be paid their wages without reckoning." [[Fasting]] constitutes half of [[sabr]]. The three forms correspond to three dimensions of struggle — with the ego in worship, with the ego in restraint, and with the ego in acceptance.

[[Sabr]] is inseparable from [[certainty]] ([[yaqin]]): the more certain the servant is of God's goodness and ultimate justice, the more completely he can endure without complaint to creation, laying his complaint before God alone. The practical sign of [[sabr]]: not showing grief to created beings while fully transparent before God. [[Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un]] should be on the tongue at every calamity. The ultimate fruit of sustained [[sabr]] is a sweetness that replaces the bitterness of endurance — a gnostic is quoted: "For forty years nothing grieved me but the fact that felicity only occurs after enduring bitterness and hardships which are in vigils." Another: "I endured the night for twenty years, then enjoyed it for twenty more."

## Chapter 29 On Gratitude

[[Shukr]] (gratitude) is the response to God's infinite, unceasing blessings. The Quran's challenge — "Should you number the favours of God, you would not be able to do so" — establishes that gratitude can never be fully discharged; but the attempt itself is the worship. [[Shukr]] has three dimensions: with the heart (acknowledging inwardly that blessings come exclusively from God, not from one's own merits), with the tongue (verbal praise — [[hamd]], [[tahmid]] — and speaking of God's blessings: "And proclaim the bounty of your Lord"), and with the limbs (using every blessing in God's obedience rather than disobedience).

The reciprocal Quranic promise: "If you are grateful I shall increase you; but if you are ungrateful, My chastisement is severe." True [[shukr]] for the intellect means using it to know God; for health, using it in God's service; for wealth, spending it in God's cause. The gnostic dimension of [[shukr]] sees every event — even apparent adversity — as ultimately from God's generosity, since a calamity that drives the servant back to God is a hidden blessing. The opposite of [[shukr]] is [[kufr al-ni'ma]] (ingratitude for blessings) — using God's gifts in disobedience — which is among the gravest forms of ingratitude.

## Chapter 30 On Renunciation

[[Zuhd]] is not hatred of the world or ascetic self-punishment — it is the turning of the heart away from the world because the hereafter is incomparably greater. Al-Haddad defines "the world" as "everything that distracts from God" — thus even acts of worship can become "worldly" if performed for reputation. The hadith: "The world is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever."

Three levels of [[zuhd]]: abstaining from the prohibited (the minimum required of every Muslim), abstaining from unnecessary licit things (the level of the sincere seeker), and abandoning everything except God (the level of the [[siddiqun]]). The highest [[zuhd]] is not poverty but freedom of heart from love of the world — a wealthy person can be a true [[zahid]] if his wealth does not occupy his heart. The love of the world is "the root of all evil," producing abandonment of obligations, commission of prohibitions, and hardening of the heart against God's reminders. The world's pleasures are compared to sweetness mixed with poison — pleasing at first, then destructive. The believer who renounces the world does not lose it: God often gives him more than those who pursue it, and he receives it without attachment — which is itself a mark of God's favor.

## Chapter 31 On Reliance on God

[[Tawakkul]] is complete dependence on God for all affairs, with the understanding that taking lawful means is itself part of [[tawakkul]] — the hadith: "Tie your camel, then put your trust in God." It is not fatalism or the abandonment of effort. Three levels are implied: the servant who takes action but knows the outcome is entirely in God's hands; the one who acts and then ceases worrying entirely, trusting like a patient trusting a wise physician; and the highest, where the servant is like a corpse in the hands of the washer — fully receptive and free of self-direction.

[[Tawakkul]] is the fruit of [[yaqin]] (certainty): only the servant who is genuinely certain of God's sufficiency, goodness, and control can genuinely rely on Him. Its practical signs: not panicking over provision, not hoarding beyond need, being at peace with whatever God sends, having concern focused on the present obligation rather than future anxieties. The Quran repeatedly commands: "Put your trust in God if you are believers." The assurance: "And whoever puts his trust in God — He shall suffice him." [[Tawakkul]] does not mean leaving one's camel untied and expecting it not to wander — it means doing everything within one's competence, then releasing the outcome entirely to God.

## Chapter 32 On Divine Love and Contentment

[[Mahabba]] (love of God) is the crown and goal of all spiritual stations. Its sign is following the [[Prophet Muhammad]] — the Quran makes this explicit: "Say: If you love God then follow me, and God will love you." True love of God manifests as: finding worship sweet rather than burdensome; feeling longing as the time for prayer approaches; preferring solitude with God over the company of creation; finding every moment of distance from God a source of grief; being indifferent to worldly praise or blame because the Beloved's pleasure alone is desired. [[Dhikr]] and [[mahabba]] are inseparable — love drives remembrance and remembrance deepens love in a self-reinforcing cycle.

[[Rida]] (contentment with God's decree) is the highest station after [[mahabba]] — in some formulations, its fullness. It is distinct from [[sabr]]: [[sabr]] is patience despite disliking what God has decreed; [[rida]] is ceasing to dislike it altogether — accepting God's choice as one's own choice. The servant in [[rida]] no longer desires anything except what God has decreed. The gnostics describe this as profound sweetness: to be fully at peace with God's will, to want only what He wants, is to live already in a taste of the Garden. In [[rida]], every act of the servant becomes an expression of worship ([[ibadah]]) in the deepest sense, since will and divine decree have converged.

## Conclusion

Al-Haddad closes the work briefly and humbly, asking God to make both himself and all believers benefit from what he has compiled. He reiterates the book's full title — *The Book of Assistance, Support, and Encouragement for Such Believers as Desire to Follow the Way of the Afterlife* — as a statement of hope rather than a description of achievement. The arc from [[yaqin]] (the first chapter) through [[mahabba]] and [[rida]] (the last) is intentional: the entire spiritual path is traversed when one begins with certainty and ends in divine love and contentment, having passed through every station of worship, ethics, and inner refinement in between. The book is an abbreviated distillation of [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]* — designed for those who cannot traverse the entire Ihya but still earnestly seek the path. As al-Haddad wrote at its outset: "Success is from God; I seek His help, depend on Him entirely, and ask Him to grant me success in being correct in my intentions and deeds."
