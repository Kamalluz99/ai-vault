> Ahmad ibn Muhammad ibn Ata'illah al-Iskandari (c. 658–709 AH / c. 1259–1309 CE) was the third master of the [[Shadhili order]] and one of the most beloved Sufi writers in Islamic history. Born in Alexandria, he studied jurisprudence before becoming a devoted disciple of [[Abu'l-Hasan al-Shadhili|Abu'l-Abbas al-Mursi]] (the successor of [[Abu'l-Hasan al-Shadhili]]). He taught in Cairo, where he also engaged in public debates. His most famous work, *al-Hikam* (The Aphorisms), is a collection of brief, luminous spiritual maxims that is among the most widely read texts in the entire Islamic spiritual tradition. He also compiled the first systematic biography of the Shadhili masters (*Lata'if al-Minan*). His saying cited by [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] in [[The Book of Assistance]] — "Because the Real knew of the presence of boredom in you, He created a variety of acts of obedience" — is characteristic of his style: finding profound wisdom in the everyday features of religious life.
