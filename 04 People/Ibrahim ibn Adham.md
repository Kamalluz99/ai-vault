> [!info]
> Ibrahim ibn Adham (c. 100–161 AH / c. 718–777 CE) was a celebrated early Muslim ascetic and Sufi figure, whose story is the Islamic archetype of the prince who renounces worldly power for the spiritual path — often compared in the literature to the story of the Buddha. Born into a royal family in Balkh (in modern-day Afghanistan), he is said to have been a prince or nobleman who experienced a profound awakening — variously described as hearing a voice from the sky while hunting, or a series of encounters with wise figures — that led him to abandon his wealth and status for a life of complete [[zuhd]] (renunciation). He spent the rest of his life in Syria and Palestine, working as a laborer, gardener, and guard to avoid taking charity. He is quoted in the Sufi literature on the relationship between eating, [[night prayer]], and the spiritual life: "If you eat well you will no longer feel obliged to pray at night and fast by day" — meaning that satiation of the body dulls the spirit's drive toward worship.
