> [!info]
> Abu Bakr Abdullah ibn Uthman al-Siddiq (51 BH–13 AH / 573–634 CE) was the closest companion of [[Prophet Muhammad]], his father-in-law, and the first Caliph of Islam (632–634 CE). He is universally regarded as the greatest of the Companions after the prophets. His epithet *al-Siddiq* (the Truthful/Affirmer) was given by the Prophet himself in reference to his immediate and complete belief in the Night Journey and Ascension (al-Isra wal-Miraj) when others doubted. His short caliphate (about two years) was marked by two supreme achievements: the compilation of the Quran into a single written volume — a project he commissioned under Zayd ibn Thabit — and the wars of apostasy (Ridda wars), in which he famously insisted on fighting those who refused to continue paying [[zakat]] after the Prophet's death, declaring he would wage war on anyone who separated prayer from zakat. This latter action is cited by [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] in [[The Book of Assistance]] as proof of zakat's equal weight to the ritual prayer.
