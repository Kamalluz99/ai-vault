> Abu Hamid Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Ghazali (450–505 AH / 1058–1111 CE), known as *Hujjat al-Islam* (the Proof of Islam), was the leading Muslim theologian, philosopher, jurist, and Sufi of the sixth Islamic century — and its designated [[mujaddid]] (renewer of religion). Born in Tus (in modern-day Iran), he studied under [[al-Juwayni]] and rose to become the principal teacher at the [[Nizamiyya Madrasa]] in Baghdad — the most prestigious academic post of his time. At the height of his fame, he experienced a profound spiritual crisis and voluntarily abandoned his position, spending ten years in solitude, travel, and inner purification before returning to a life of teaching.
>
> The *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) is his magnum opus — a monumental forty-volume work synthesizing Islamic jurisprudence, theology, spiritual psychology, and Sufi practice. It remains the single most comprehensive manual of Islamic spiritual formation ever written. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] considered al-Ghazali's writings — especially the Ihya — the surest and most comprehensive guide for the seekers of the path, and explicitly described most of his own books as abbreviated adaptations of the Ihya for those who cannot traverse it in full. His other major works include *Tahafut al-Falasifa* (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), *Ihya* (abridged), *Minhaj al-'Abidin*, *Kimiya-yi Sa'adat* (The Alchemy of Happiness), and the *al-Risala al-Qudsiyya* (Jerusalem Epistle) on creed.
>
> Al-Ghazali was also a trained jurist in the Shafi'i school and a theologian in the [[Ash'ari school|Ash'ari]] tradition. He wrote devastating critiques of the Muslim philosophers of his era while also deeply engaging with Sufi practice. He died in Tus in 1111 CE.
