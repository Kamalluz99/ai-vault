> Imam Abdallah ibn Alawi al-Haddad Ba-'Alawi al-Husayni (1044–1132 AH / ~1634–1720 CE) was the [[mujaddid]] (renewer of religion) of the twelfth Islamic century and one of the most celebrated scholars and [[gnostics]] of the [[Ba-Alawi]] tradition. Born in [[Tarim]], [[Hadramaut]], in the southerly Arabian peninsula, he became blind in infancy yet grew into a figure of towering spiritual authority. His lineage is traced directly to [[Prophet Muhammad]] through [[Imam al-Husayn]], placing him among the [[Alawi sadat]] — a family that had produced generations of scholars, saints, and summoners to God's path.
>
> Al-Haddad spent his entire life in [[Tarim]] and the surrounding region, teaching, writing, and guiding seekers. He is formally recognised as the Mujaddid of the twelfth century — fulfilling the prophetic promise that God would raise such a renewer in every century. His writings are concise, clear, and deliberately uncontroversial, aimed at establishing the firmest foundations for [[iman]] (faith), [[certainty]] ([[yaqin]]), and virtuous practice in ordinary Muslims. Most of his books are abbreviated and accessible adaptations of [[Imam al-Ghazali]]'s vast *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]*. He also wrote a celebrated [[Diwan]] (collection of poetry).
>
> His most widely-read works include *[[The Book of Assistance]]* (*Kitab al-'Awn wa al-Ma'una*), *The Book of Assistance in Following the Sunna*, *The Lives of Man* (*Hayat al-Insan*), and *Gifts for the Seeker*. These remain continuously in print across the Islamic world. He died on the eve of the seventh of Dhu'l-Qa'da, 1132 AH, having spent his life bringing people to their Lord through oral and written teaching and exemplary conduct. He was buried in a simple grave in the cemetery at [[Tarim]].
