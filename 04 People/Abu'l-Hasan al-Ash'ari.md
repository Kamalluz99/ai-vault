> Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Ismail al-Ash'ari (260–324 AH / 874–936 CE) was the founder of the [[Ash'ari school]] of Islamic theology (*kalam*) — the dominant school of Sunni Islamic creed to this day. Born in Basra, he was initially a student of the Mu'tazilite theologian al-Jubbai but dramatically parted ways with Mu'tazilism around the age of 40, reportedly announcing his break in a public mosque. He then devoted himself to systematizing the creed of the early Muslims (the *salaf*) — the beliefs of the Companions and the best of the Followers — using the rational and philosophical tools of the *kalam* tradition, but in defense of traditional theology rather than in departure from it. His foundational works include *Maqalat al-Islamiyyin* (The Doctrines of the Muslims) and *al-Ibana 'an Usul al-Diyana* (The Elucidation of the Foundations of Religion). The [[Ash'ari school]] became the creed of the vast majority of Sunni Muslims, including virtually all Sufis, and [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] explicitly identifies it as the creed of the [[Ba-Alawi]] tradition and the "party of salvation."
