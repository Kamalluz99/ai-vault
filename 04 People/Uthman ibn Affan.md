> [!info]
> Uthman ibn Affan (c. 44 BH–35 AH / c. 576–656 CE) was the third Caliph of Islam (644–656 CE) and a leading Companion of [[Prophet Muhammad]], who married two of the Prophet's daughters (Ruqayya, then Umm Kulthum) — earning him the title *Dhu al-Nurayn* (Possessor of Two Lights). Born into the wealthy Umayyad clan of the Quraysh, he embraced Islam early and used his wealth generously in God's path. His caliphate is most remembered for two major achievements: the standardization of the written Quran — commissioning the authoritative copy (*Mushaf Uthmani*) from which all Qurans in the world today are copied — and significant expansion of the Islamic state. He was assassinated in Medina in 35 AH during a period of civil strife, dying as a martyr while reciting the Quran. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] cites him in [[The Book of Assistance]] as one of the four Rightly-Guided Caliphs whose innovations (such as the standardized Quran and a second call to Friday prayer) exemplify "good innovation" in religion.
