> Abu Yazid Tayfur ibn Isa al-Bistami (c. 188–261 AH / c. 804–874 CE), known as Bayazid al-Bistami, was one of the most celebrated and controversial Sufi masters of the early period. Born in Bistam (in modern-day Iran), he is renowned for his *shatahat* — ecstatic utterances expressing states of spiritual absorption that, taken literally, scandalised the exoteric scholars of his time. His most famous such utterance — "Glory be to me! How great is my majesty!" — became the classic example of the kind of speech that emerges in states of extreme mystical absorption (*[[fana]]*), which the Sufi tradition interprets as the voice of the Real speaking through an extinguished self rather than the speech of the ego. Despite the apparent controversy of such utterances, [[Al-Junayd]] and other sober masters defended him, and he became a revered figure in virtually all Sufi lineages. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] relates the story of Abu Yazid walking away from a man reputed to be a saint after seeing him spit on a mosque wall — illustrating that al-Bistami himself upheld the [[Sunna]] above all and measured sainthood by its adherence.
