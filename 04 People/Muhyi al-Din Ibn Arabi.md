> Muhammad ibn Ali ibn Muhammad ibn Arabi al-Hatimi al-Ta'i (560–638 AH / 1165–1240 CE), known as *al-Shaykh al-Akbar* (the Greatest Master), was the most prolific and metaphysically ambitious Sufi thinker in Islamic history. Born in Murcia, al-Andalus (Spain), he traveled extensively throughout the Islamic world before settling in Damascus, where he died and is buried. His most celebrated works — *al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya* (The Meccan Illuminations, an encyclopedic work of several thousand pages) and *Fusus al-Hikam* (The Bezels of Wisdom) — present a comprehensive metaphysical system centered on the concept of *wahdat al-wujud* (the unity of being). His ideas have been enormously influential in all Sufi traditions but have also been deeply controversial: his terminology is technical and difficult, and his works are easily misread by the unprepared as suggesting pantheism, [[hulul]] (divine incarnation), or [[ittihad]] (the fusion of creature and Creator) — concepts that constitute disbelief if taken literally. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] cautions beginners against reading his works without a qualified guide, while quoting him approvingly on other matters (e.g., the story of the Moroccan worshipper in Chapter 12 of [[The Book of Assistance]]).
