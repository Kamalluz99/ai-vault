> Sahl ibn Abd Allah al-Tustari (200–283 AH / 818–896 CE) was one of the most prominent early Sufi masters, known for his asceticism, his rigorous adherence to the [[Sunna]], and his subtle Quranic commentary. Born in Tustar (Shushtar) in southwestern Iran, he is said to have fasted continuously for extended periods and to have reduced his food intake to a remarkable minimum. He was a teacher of [[Al-Junayd|al-Hallaj]] (the controversial mystic executed in Baghdad). His Quranic commentary (*Tafsir al-Tustari*) is one of the earliest surviving Sufi tafsirs and is valued for its spiritual depth. He is most quoted in the Sufi literature for his emphasis on combining outward [[Sunna]] adherence with inward spiritual realization — a formula he expressed in the saying cited by [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]]: "There is no helper but God, no guide but the Messenger of God, no provision but [[taqwa]], and no work but to have [[fortitude]] in these things."
