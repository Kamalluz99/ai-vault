> [!note]- Article: How to Overcome Addiction through Faith — Ibn Al-Qayyim's Rehab Program
> **Dr. Zohair Abdul-Rahman** — applies Ibn Al-Qayyim's classical model of the heart (*qalb*) and spiritual disease to contemporary addiction, specifically the overconsumption of curated digital stimuli (social media, pornography, endless content). Core argument: addiction is a *qalb* disorder, not merely a behavioural one, and Islamic spiritual medicine — tawbah, muraqabah, dhikr, and fasting — addresses the root, not just the symptom. The "rehab program" is structured around Ibn Al-Qayyim's *Madarij al-Salikin* framework of spiritual stations.

> [!note]- Article: Female Scholars in Islamic History — Revisiting Their Pivotal Role
> **Jude Elziq** (~28 min read) — challenges the narrative that women were marginal in Islamic intellectual history. Documents women who held ijazahs (scholarly licenses), taught hadith to major male scholars, and led circles in the early centuries of Islam. Key cases include Aisha (ra) as the foundational example, through to medieval muhaddithat (hadith transmitters) like Karima al-Marwaziyya. Argument: erasure of female scholarship is a modern and colonial artefact, not a classical Islamic norm.
> - Category: History

> [!note]- Article: Hindutva and Anti-Muslim Violence — How to Navigate Hindu Supremacy
> **Safa Ahmed** (~71 min read) — comprehensive analysis of Hindutva as a political ideology distinct from Hinduism as a religion, tracing its origins through Savarkar and the RSS to its current state-backed form under Modi's BJP. Covers the mechanics of anti-Muslim violence: mob lynchings, ghar wapsi (forced reconversions), and institutional marginalisation. The Islamic response section draws on fiqh of minorities, prophetic precedents for living under hostile governance, and the obligation of advocacy and solidarity. Long-form academic tone.
> - Category: Politics & Practical Theology

> [!note]- Article: Why Silence Is Not Neutral — The Bystander Effect and Moral Responsibility in Islam
> **Dr. Jibran Khokhar** (~7 min) — uses the social psychology concept of the bystander effect (diffusion of responsibility in crowds) as a lens on Islamic texts commanding *amr bil ma'ruf wa nahy 'an al-munkar* (enjoining good and forbidding wrong). Central point: Islam explicitly dismantles the bystander logic — the hadith "whoever sees an evil, let him change it" assigns individual obligation regardless of who else is present. Applied to contemporary Muslim passivity in the face of injustice, online and offline.
