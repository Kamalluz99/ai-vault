*Bid'ah* (بدعة) — innovation in religion — refers to introducing into religious practice something not grounded in the [[Quran]], the [[Sunna]], or the established consensus of the early Muslim community. The Prophet's warning — "Every innovation is an error, and every error is in the Fire" — is the primary textual basis for the concept. Classical scholars, however, debated its scope and classified innovations into categories. [[Imam al-Ghazali]] and [[Imam al-Nawawi]], among others, distinguished at minimum between blameworthy innovations (which contradict established religious norms) and acceptable practices that accord with the general spirit of the religion even if not explicitly established by the Prophet. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] in [[The Book of Assistance]] (Chapter 10) identifies three types: (1) *good innovation* — practices introduced by the Rightly-Guided Caliphs in conformity with the Book and Sunna, such as compiling the Quran into one volume or organizing the Tarawih prayer in congregation; (2) *blameworthy only from the standpoint of renunciation* — excessive indulgence in licit comfort; and (3) *absolutely blameworthy innovation* — that which contradicts the provisions of the Book and Sunna or the consensus of the community. See also: [[Sunna]], [[Ash'ari school]].
