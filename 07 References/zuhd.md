*Zuhd* (زهد) — renunciation, detachment from the world — is not the hatred of the world or the rejection of its lawful enjoyments, but the freeing of the heart from attachment to it because the hereafter is incomparably greater. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] defines "the world" (*dunya*) in [[The Book of Assistance]] as "everything that distracts from God" — a definition that encompasses even acts of worship performed for reputation or worldly benefit. The hadith is cited: "The world is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever."

Three levels of *zuhd* are identified in classical literature: (1) abstaining from the clearly prohibited — the minimum required of every Muslim; (2) abstaining from the unnecessary licit — the level of the sincere seeker (*murid*); and (3) abandoning everything except God — the level of the *[[siddiqun]]* (the Truthful). The highest *zuhd* is not poverty but interior freedom from love of the world: a wealthy person can be a true *zahid* if his wealth does not occupy his heart. [[Ibrahim ibn Adham]] — the prince-ascetic — is the archetype of complete *zuhd* in the Sufi tradition. See also: [[tawakkul]], [[mahabba]], [[rida]].
