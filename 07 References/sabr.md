*Sabr* (صبر) — fortitude, patience, steadfast endurance — is one of the central virtues in Islamic ethics and spirituality, mentioned in the Quran over ninety times. Classical scholars identify three types: (1) patience in obeying God — sustaining acts of worship and righteousness against the ego's preference for ease (considered the most noble); (2) patience in abstaining from what God has forbidden — resisting desire and temptation; and (3) patience in enduring what God has decreed — bearing calamities, hardships, illness, and loss with composure.

[[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] dedicates Chapter 28 of [[The Book of Assistance]] to *sabr*, noting that fasting constitutes "half of fortitude" and that the Quran promises: "Indeed those who have fortitude will be paid their wages without reckoning" (39:10). The practical sign of *sabr* is not complaining about God's decree to created beings while remaining transparent before God alone — "I complain of my grief and sorrow to God alone" (Quran 12:86). Sustained *sabr* eventually transforms: the bitterness of endurance is replaced by the sweetness of proximity to God. *Sabr* is inseparable from [[yaqin]] (certainty) — the more certain the servant is of God's ultimate goodness and justice, the more completely he can bear without complaint.
