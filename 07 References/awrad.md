*Awrad* (أوراد, singular: *wird*) are regular, scheduled devotional practices assigned to a specific time of day or night and performed with consistency. The term *wird* literally means "a watering place" — the regular spiritual nourishment a servant returns to. In Sufi tradition, a shaykh typically assigns a student a personal *wird* of specific invocations, prayers, and Quran recitations to be performed at set times. More broadly, any consistent devotional routine — morning adhkar, night prayer, daily Quran reading — constitutes a *wird*. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] dedicates Chapter 5 of [[The Book of Assistance]] to the subject, teaching that awrad should be varied (not confined to one type), moderate in quantity (chosen for what one can sustain, not what one might manage on a good day), and performed at specifically allocated times. His master [[Abd al-Rahman al-Saqqaf]] is quoted: "Whoever has no *wird* is a *qird* (monkey)" — meaning the servant without structured devotional routine has no real spiritual standing. The five main forms of *awrad* are: supererogatory prayers, [[Quran]] recitation, knowledge acquisition, [[dhikr]] (invocation), and [[fikr]] (reflection).
