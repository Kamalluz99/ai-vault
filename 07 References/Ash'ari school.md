The Ash'ari school (*al-madhhab al-Ash'ari*) is the dominant school of Islamic theology (*kalam*) among Sunni Muslims, founded by [[Abu'l-Hasan al-Ash'ari]] (d. 324 AH / 936 CE). It represents a middle path between the strict textualism of the Hanbalis (who rejected rational theology) and the rationalism of the Mu'tazilites (who subordinated revelation to reason): it accepts the use of rational and philosophical tools in theology, but deploys them in defense of the traditional creed of the early Muslims (*salaf*) rather than in departure from it. Key Ash'ari doctrines include the affirmation of God's Essence and Attributes (without anthropomorphism or negation), the createdness of the Quran as a physical object (while affirming the uncreated nature of God's eternal speech), the affirmation of God's will and power as encompassing all things, and the authoritative status of the Companions' consensus. The Ash'ari school is the creed of the vast majority of Sunni scholars, jurists, and Sufis — including the [[Ba-Alawi]] tradition of [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]]. The parallel [[Maturidi school]] — named after Abu Mansur al-Maturidi of Samarkand — reaches essentially identical conclusions through a slightly different methodology and is considered equally valid by Ash'ari scholars.
