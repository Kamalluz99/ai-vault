*Ma'rifa* (معرفة) — gnosis, direct experiential knowledge of God — is distinguished in the Sufi tradition from *'ilm* (theoretical, rational, or transmitted knowledge). Where *'ilm* is knowledge *about* God acquired through study, argument, and transmission, *ma'rifa* is knowledge *of* God acquired through direct inner experience — the fruit of purification, sustained [[dhikr]], [[muraqaba]], and God's direct disclosure ([[kashf]]). The one who has attained *ma'rifa* is called an *'arif* (gnostic).

[[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] uses the concept throughout [[The Book of Assistance]] to define the ultimate goal of all the practices he prescribes. In Chapter 7, he specifies that beneficial knowledge is that which increases one's *ma'rifa* of God. In Chapter 11, he explicitly states that *ma'rifa* "cannot be obtained through [[kalam]] (speculative theology)" but only through the path: *taqwa*, meditation, soul-discipline, [[dhikr]], and sustained reflection. The great Sufi masters — especially [[Imam al-Ghazali]] in the *[[Ihya Ulum al-Din]]* — treat *ma'rifa* as the highest form of knowledge a human being can attain, and the entire Sufi method of inner purification is ultimately aimed at its realization. See also: [[kashf]], [[fana]], [[siddiqun]], [[yaqin]].
