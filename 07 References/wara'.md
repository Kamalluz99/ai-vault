*Wara'* (ورع) — scrupulousness, pious caution — is the practice of abstaining not only from the clearly prohibited (*haram*) but also from suspect (*mashbuh*) things, out of vigilance for the soundness of one's religion and worship. The term implies a heightened sensitivity to the lawfulness of what one consumes, earns, wears, and uses, grounded in the understanding that unlawful or suspect inputs corrupt worship from within. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] calls *wara'* "the pillar of religion" in Chapter 21 of [[The Book of Assistance]]. The hadith is cited: "The one who guards himself against suspect things has protected his religion and his honour; the one who falls into suspect things will fall into the prohibited."

Classical scholars classify suspect things on a spectrum — from items nearly certainly prohibited to items nearly certainly licit — and teach that *wara'* demands abstaining from all suspect things even when an apparent legal license might exist. The Companions are cited as having left "seventy licit avenues for fear of falling into the illicit." *Wara'* applies to food and drink (most critically, since these form the body's energy for worship), to earnings and transactions, to clothing, and to every other means the servant uses.
