*Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) is the magnum opus of [[Imam al-Ghazali]], written in the late fifth Islamic century (~1095–1096 CE) after he withdrew from his prestigious teaching post in Baghdad following a spiritual crisis. It is widely regarded as the most comprehensive manual of Islamic spirituality ever compiled — synthesizing Islamic jurisprudence, theology, psychology of the soul, and Sufi practice into a unified whole. The work runs to approximately forty books (organized into four quarters: acts of worship, social customs, destructive vices, and saving virtues) and systematically covers the outer and inner dimensions of every major aspect of Muslim life. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] described it as the premier reference for those seeking to reform their souls and unify outward practice with inward sincerity, and explicitly modeled most of his own writings — including [[The Book of Assistance]] — as abbreviated and accessible distillations of the Ihya for Muslims who cannot traverse the full work.
