Tarim (تريم) is a historic town in the Wadi [[Hadramaut]] of southeastern Yemen, widely regarded as one of the great centers of Islamic scholarship and spirituality in the Arabian Peninsula. It is home to an exceptional density of mosques — traditionally said to include 360 mosques — and has been described as producing more Islamic scholars per capita than almost any other city in the world. The town is the heartland of the [[Alawi sadat]] ([[Ba-Alawi]]) tradition: the descendants of [[Prophet Muhammad]] through [[Imam al-Husayn]] who settled in Hadramaut from the tenth century CE onward and built a continuous civilization of Islamic learning, [[gnosis]], and missionary outreach that spread Islam throughout East Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. [[Imam Abdallah al-Haddad]] — the [[mujaddid]] of the twelfth Islamic century — was born, lived, and died in Tarim, and is buried in its cemetery. The town is visited by pilgrims seeking the blessing (*baraka*) of its many scholars and saints.
